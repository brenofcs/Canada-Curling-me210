Project Overview

This project, set out by the 2026 ME210 teaching team, was to build an autonomous curling robot — a DROID that starts in a random orientation, finds its way down the curling sheet, launches pucks toward the house, and does all of this without embarrassing itself in front of family, friends, and the teaching staff.

The challenge combined navigation, sensing, actuation, and strategy under tight constraints: a small footprint, full autonomy, a limited budget, and the requirement to "beat the brick." Over a 4-week sprint, our robot evolved from ambitious ideas into a working system with a clear strategy, integrated sensing, and a repeatable puck-shooting mechanism.

Rules & Requirements

  • Fully autonomous — no human interaction during a run except legal reloading
  • Start in a randomized orientation with three pucks loaded
  • Two-minute run time; max footprint 13×13 in starting, 20×20 in deployed
  • Front of starting footprint cannot cross the hog line
  • Arduino Uno control, untethered battery power, external e-stop, fuse required
  • Total material cost under $200
  • Minimum: score ≥ 3 points in 2 minutes ("beat the brick")

Design Strategy

From day one, our goal was simple: beat the brick. We designed around the curling sheet's center line and divided the robot's behavior into a clear sequence of stages:

  1. Orient toward the house using IR beacon detection
  2. Drive forward to fully exit the starting zone
  3. Turn 90° and drive laterally to find the center line
  4. Align to the center line and follow it toward the hog line
  5. Stop at the hog line and dispense all three pucks

Because the robot began in a random orientation inside a tape-bounded starting zone, we needed to fully exit before beginning any tape sensing. IR beacon detection oriented the robot toward the house first, then the robot exited, turned 90°, and swept laterally until the center line was found.

Mechanical Design

The robot combined a laser-cut Duron chassis with 3D-printed PLA components. Duron was chosen for its light weight, stiffness, and availability in the lab.

Drivetrain

Two DC motors in a differential drive configuration, stabilized by two ball-transfer rollers. A software trim constant of 0.8 on the right motor corrected a physical speed mismatch, enabling straight-line travel without feedback control.

IR Tower

A 3D-printed PLA tower elevated two phototransistors above the chassis with a barrier between them to isolate left and right beacon signals. The tower also mounted the battery packs, keeping the center of mass low and centered.

Puck Dispenser

A rack-and-pinion mechanism driven by a third DC motor extended a puck arm forward on reaching the hog line, pushing all three pucks into the scoring zone.

Electrical Design

All sensing, motor driving, and power distribution ran through a single breadboard mounted on the chassis.

Power

Provided battery packs with an in-line fuse and an externally accessible emergency stop switch wired in series with the main power rail.

DC Motor Drivers

Three H-bridge drivers provided independent PWM control of each motor. A software trim constant of 0.8 on the right drive motor corrected a physical speed mismatch for straight-line travel.

Tape Sensors

Four reflectance sensors: center mid (pin 2), rear (A5), and two front hog-line sensors (pins 3 and 4). Each outputs HIGH over black tape, LOW otherwise.

IR Phototransistors

Two phototransistors on pins A0 and A1 detected the 909 Hz IR beacons. The ADC prescaler was reduced to divide-by-16 to achieve the sampling rate required by the Goertzel algorithm.

Software & Finite State Machine

The robot's behavior was implemented as an 8-state FSM in the Arduino main loop — each run stage mapped to one self-contained, independently testable state.

STATE_FIND_BEARING
Rotates until both IR sensors simultaneously detect the 909 Hz beacon, indicating the robot faces the house.
STATE_EXIT_START
Drives forward for 4000 ms to fully clear the starting zone bounding tape.
STATE_TURN_RIGHT_90
Timed 1000 ms right rotation to face perpendicular to the center line.
STATE_FIND_CENTERLINE
Drives laterally until the center mid sensor detects the tape.
STATE_ALIGN_REAR
Rotates until the rear sensor also detects tape, confirming parallel alignment toward the house.
STATE_ADVANCE_TO_HOG
Drives forward until a front hog-line sensor triggers, stopping just short of the restricted zone.
STATE_LAUNCH
Extends the rack-and-pinion arm for 2000 ms, pushing all three pucks into the house.
STATE_DONE
Stops all motors. Terminal state.

IR Beacon Detection: Goertzel Algorithm

Instead of raw thresholding — susceptible to ambient light — we implemented a Goertzel algorithm, an efficient single-frequency DFT, to detect specifically the 909 Hz beacon signal. 50 ADC samples were collected per sensor at ~9090 samples/sec, DC offset was removed, and the Goertzel recurrence computed power at frequency bin k=5. A threshold of 1000.0 determined detection.

Results

25s
Our robot completed its mission in just 25 seconds — the 5th team in the class to get checked off, roughly 6 days before the deadline. It reliably navigated from a random orientation and delivered all three pucks in a single run.

The project required only one late night in the lab to achieve final integration. The robot consistently beat the brick requirement (3 points in 2 minutes) and demonstrated the value of keeping the strategy simple, the code modular, and the mechanical design practical.

Conclusion

By committing early to a clear and simple strategy — IR beacon detection to orient, tape sensing to navigate, and a rack-and-pinion arm to score — we avoided the scope creep that derails many ME210 projects. Every design decision was made in service of one goal: beat the brick.

The most valuable lesson was the importance of modular design. Building discrete, independently debuggable subsystems meant that final integration was assembly, not discovery. Sometimes the best engineering decision is knowing when you're done.
