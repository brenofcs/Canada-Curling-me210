Project Overview

This project, set out by the 2026 ME210 teaching team, was to build an autonomous curling robot — a DROID that starts in a random orientation, finds its way down the curling sheet, launches pucks toward the house, and does all of this without embarrassing itself in front of family, friends, and the teaching staff. The challenge combined navigation, sensing, actuation, and strategy under tight constraints: a small footprint, full autonomy, a limited budget, and the requirement to "beat the brick."

Over a 4-week sprint, our robot evolved from ambitious ideas and questionable assumptions into a working system with a clear strategy, integrated sensing, and a repeatable puck-shooting mechanism. This page walks through that full process.

Rules & Requirements

  • Fully autonomous — no human interaction during a run except legal reloading
  • Start in a randomized orientation with three pucks loaded
  • Two-minute run time
  • Max starting footprint: 13 in × 13 in; max deployed: 20 in × 20 in; max height: 12 in
  • Front of starting footprint cannot cross the hog line (requires an expanding mechanism)
  • Arduino Uno-based control, untethered battery power, external e-stop, fuse required
  • Total material cost under $200
  • Minimum requirement: score at least 3 points in 2 minutes ("beat the brick")

Design Strategy

From day one, our goal was simple: beat the brick. We designed around the curling sheet's center line, which runs straight toward the house. Our strategy broke the robot's behavior into a clear sequence of stages:

  1. Orient toward the house using IR beacon detection
  2. Drive forward to fully exit the starting zone
  3. Turn 90° and drive laterally to find the center line
  4. Align to the center line and follow it toward the hog line
  5. Stop at the hog line and dispense all three pucks

Mechanical Design

The robot combined a laser-cut Duron chassis with 3D-printed PLA components. Duron was chosen for its light weight, stiffness, and availability in the lab.

Drivetrain

Two DC motors in a differential drive configuration, with two ball-transfer rollers for stability. Simple, practical, and sturdy enough to serve as the foundation for everything else.

IR Tower

A 3D-printed PLA tower elevated two phototransistors above the chassis. A barrier between them isolated left and right beacon signals. The tower also served as the battery mounting point, keeping the center of mass low and centered.

Puck Dispenser

A rack-and-pinion mechanism driven by a third DC motor extended a puck arm forward. On reaching the hog line, the arm extended and pushed all three pucks into the scoring zone.

Electrical Design

All sensing, motor driving, and power distribution ran through a single breadboard mounted on the chassis.

Power

Provided battery packs, a fuse in the power line, and an externally accessible emergency stop switch wired in series with the main rail.

DC Motor Drivers

Three H-bridge drivers gave independent PWM control of each motor. A software trim constant of 0.8 was applied to the right motor to compensate for a physical speed mismatch, allowing straight-line travel without feedback control.

Tape Sensors

Four reflectance sensors: one center mid-sensor and one rear sensor for center-line detection, and two front sensors for hog-line detection. Each output digital HIGH over black tape, LOW otherwise.

IR Phototransistors

Two phototransistors on analog pins A0 and A1 detected the 909 Hz IR beacons. The ADC prescaler was reduced (divide-by-16) to increase sampling rate for the Goertzel algorithm.

Software & Finite State Machine

The robot's behavior was implemented as an 8-state FSM running in the Arduino main loop, giving a modular, debuggable structure where each run stage mapped directly to one state.

STATE_FIND_BEARING — Rotates until both IR sensors simultaneously detect the 909 Hz beacon, indicating the robot faces the house.
STATE_EXIT_START — Drives forward for 4000 ms to fully clear the starting zone.
STATE_TURN_RIGHT_90 — Timed 1000 ms right rotation to face perpendicular to the center line.
STATE_FIND_CENTERLINE — Drives laterally until the center mid sensor detects the tape.
STATE_ALIGN_REAR — Rotates until the rear sensor also detects tape, confirming alignment.
STATE_ADVANCE_TO_HOG — Drives forward until a front hog-line sensor triggers.
STATE_LAUNCH — Extends the rack-and-pinion arm for 2000 ms, delivering all three pucks.
STATE_DONE — Stops all motors. Terminal state.

IR Beacon Detection: Goertzel Algorithm

Instead of raw thresholding (susceptible to ambient light), we implemented a Goertzel algorithm — an efficient single-frequency DFT — to detect specifically the 909 Hz beacon signal. 50 samples were collected per sensor at ~9090 samples/sec, DC offset was removed, and the Goertzel recurrence computed the power at frequency bin k=5. A threshold of 1000.0 determined detection.

Results

Our robot completed its mission in 25 seconds, making us the 5th team in the class to get checked off — roughly 6 days before the deadline. It reliably started in a random orientation, found the beacon, navigated to the center line, advanced to the hog line, and delivered all three pucks in a single run. Only one late night in the lab was needed for final integration.

Conclusion

By committing early to a clear and simple strategy — IR beacon detection to orient, tape sensing to navigate, and a rack-and-pinion arm to score — we avoided the scope creep that derails many ME210 projects. Every design decision was made in service of one goal: beat the brick.

The most valuable lesson was the importance of modular design. Building discrete, independently debuggable subsystems meant that final integration was assembly, not discovery. Sometimes the best engineering decision is knowing when you're done.
